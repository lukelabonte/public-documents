🍽️ How Meals Work ▼

Each dog eats twice per day — morning and evening. Every meal has two parts: Victor kibble weighed on the kitchen scale, plus a scoop of pre-made topper from the fridge. The topper is cooked white rice mixed with Kirkland organic mixed vegetables.

Mix the kibble and topper together in the bowl so the picky ones can't just eat the rice and leave the kibble behind. A quick stir with a spoon is enough.

Why is the bowl heavier than before?
The total grams per bowl went up compared to straight kibble + wet food, but the calories are the same. The mixed veggies and rice have water in them, so they weigh more per calorie than dehydrated kibble. They're not eating more food — it just weighs more.

Supplements

All three dogs get Cosequin (glucosamine/chondroitin joint supplement) with their morning meal. Cali and Buddy each get 1 chew; Odin gets 2 chews. Since we reduced the kibble by 25%, they're getting less of the built-in glucosamine from the Victor, so the Cosequin is even more important now.

🍳 Weekly Batch Cook — Step by Step ▼

Cook once a week. The whole batch takes about 30 minutes and makes 7 days of topper for all 3 dogs. Store in the fridge.

What You're Making (7-Day Batch)

Ingredient Weekly Amount What That Looks Like
White rice (dry) ~1,980g (~4.4 lbs) ~10 cups dry → ~5,940g cooked
Kirkland Organic Mixed Vegetables (frozen) ~2,545g (~5.6 lbs) ~1.1 bags (5 lb bag)
Total cooked topper ~8,485g — enough for all 3 dogs for 7 days

Instructions

1

Cook the rice

Rinse ~10 cups of dry white rice. Cook in a rice cooker or large pot with the standard 1:2 ratio of rice to water. This will make about 5,940g of cooked rice. No salt, no butter, no seasoning.

2

Thaw the mixed vegetables

Thaw ~5.6 lbs of Kirkland Organic Mixed Vegetables (carrots, corn, peas, green beans) — microwave or run under warm water. No cooking needed. You want about 2,545g thawed.

3

Mix it together

In a large mixing bowl or pot, combine the cooked rice and thawed vegetables. The ratio is 70% rice, 30% veggies by weight — roughly 5,940g rice to 2,545g veggies. Stir until evenly distributed.

4

Store it

Transfer into 1–2 large airtight containers and refrigerate. This is the topper for the entire week. Just scoop from the container each meal, weigh it on the scale, and add it to the kibble bowl.

Tip
Let the rice cool to room temperature before mixing with the vegetables, then refrigerate. Don't put hot rice straight in the fridge — it'll create excess moisture and get mushy faster.
📦 Storage & Portioning Tips ▼

How to Store the Topper

Keep the full batch in 1–2 large airtight containers in the fridge. Wide, shallow containers (like a large Rubbermaid or Pyrex) work better than deep ones — easier to scoop from and the food stays fresher because less air gets trapped.

The topper lasts 5–6 days in the fridge comfortably. If you're worried about the tail end of the week, you can split the batch in half and freeze the second half, then move it to the fridge on day 3 or 4 to thaw overnight.

Per-Meal Portioning

No need to pre-portion into individual containers. Just use the kitchen scale at each meal:

  1. Place the bowl on the scale, tare to zero
  2. Scoop in the correct kibble weight
  3. Tare again
  4. Scoop in the correct topper weight
  5. Stir together with a spoon
For house sitters
If you're prepping meals for someone else to feed, you can pre-portion into individual containers or ziplock bags labeled with each dog's name. That way the sitter just grabs a container per dog per meal — no scale needed.

Freezing

The topper freezes well for up to 3 months. If you want to cook two batches at once every two weeks instead of weekly, that works. Freeze in daily or half-week portions and thaw in the fridge overnight.

🔄 Transition Plan — First Two Weeks ▼

Don't go from full kibble + wet food to the new plan overnight. Transition over about 10 days to avoid upset stomachs.

Days Kibble Wet Food Topper
1–3 Full amount (current) Half Small scoop (~50g)
4–6 Reduce by ~10% Quarter ~100g per dog
7–9 Down to 75% target None Full amount
10+ Final amount None Final amount
Watch for
Loose stools or digestive upset during the transition. If you see it, slow down and stay at the current step for a few more days. Rice is actually easy on dog stomachs (vets recommend bland rice diets for upset stomachs), so this transition should be smoother than switching between kibble brands.
📊 Nutrition Notes ▼

At the 75% kibble level, the Victor food still covers all essential vitamins and minerals. The topper adds calories, fiber, and digestible carbohydrates. Here's what each ingredient brings:

  • White rice (70%): Easy-to-digest carbohydrate energy. Dogs digest white rice better than brown. Gentle on stomachs.
  • Mixed vegetables — carrots, corn, peas, green beans (30%): Fiber, beta-carotene, vitamin K, manganese, and some plant protein. The variety adds good micronutrient coverage.

Why Not a Supplement?

At 75% kibble, you don't need a separate multivitamin. The Victor formula already has glucosamine, chondroitin, probiotics, omega fatty acids, taurine, and a full vitamin/mineral panel. At 75%, that's still enough to cover daily requirements. If you ever drop below 70% kibble, you'd want to add a dog multivitamin supplement like Balance IT.

Calorie Match

This plan delivers approximately the same daily calories each dog was getting on the old kibble + wet food plan. All three dogs are maintaining their weight well on the current amounts, so no adjustment should be needed. If any dog starts gaining or losing weight, adjust the topper amount up or down by 15–20g per meal.

Weight check
Weigh the dogs monthly or do the rib test — you should be able to feel their ribs easily with light pressure but not see them. If you notice weight changes, adjust the topper portion first (it's the easiest variable to change).
✨ Optional Add-ins & Variations ▼

The base recipe (rice, eggs, peas) is your staple. But if you want to rotate things for variety, here are safe add-ins:

  • Eggs (hard-boiled or scrambled): If you want to add extra protein, a few hard-boiled or scrambled eggs per batch is an easy option. Chop them up and mix in. One of the most bioavailable protein sources for dogs.
  • Frozen broccoli: Fine in small amounts. Costco has the Kirkland Organic 4×16oz for $9.99. You can swap some of the mixed veggies for broccoli if you want variety. Don't overdo it — too much broccoli can cause gas.
  • Bone broth (no sodium/no onion): Mix a splash into the topper for extra flavor on days the dogs seem uninterested. Make sure it has zero onion and zero garlic — check the label carefully.
  • Ground turkey: You can add it to a batch for extra protein. The Costco fresh ground turkey (93/7, 4×1.67lb for $25.99) works — cook it through, drain, and mix in. It's more expensive per calorie than eggs, so treat it as an occasional rotation, not a staple.
  • Spinach: Small amounts are fine, raw or lightly steamed. Good source of iron. Don't make it a main ingredient — oxalates in large quantities aren't great for dogs.
  • Plain pumpkin puree: Great for digestion. A spoonful mixed in can help with loose stools or picky eating.
Never Add
Onions, garlic, grapes, raisins, chocolate, xylitol (sugar-free sweetener), macadamia nuts, or anything with added salt or seasoning.

🍽️ How Meals Work ▼

Both cats eat twice a day — morning and evening. Each cat has their own microchip-activated feeder that only opens for them, so you don't need to worry about them stealing each other's dry food. The lid opens automatically when the right cat approaches.

Dry Food — Feeders

Each feeder holds dry kibble. Just top them off when they're running low — there's no strict per-meal amount for dry. The feeders control access, not quantity.

  • Maui's feeder: Hill's Science Diet Adult Sensitive Stomach & Skin Grain-Free Salmon & Yellow Pea
  • Moki's feeder: Royal Canin Veterinary Gastrointestinal (the prescription bag — don't substitute)

Wet Food — Per Meal

Each cat gets ~1 can of wet food per meal (morning and evening). Open a can, put it in their bowl next to their feeder. Flavors are flexible — we keep several on hand.

  • Maui: Her current favorite is Fancy Feast Medleys Shredded White Meat Chicken Fare. She'll eat others too — Fancy Feast Medleys White Meat Chicken Primavera, Purina ONE Chicken, etc.
  • Moki: Whatever he'll eat — see the pickiness note below. We keep Fancy Feast Medleys, Purina ONE Beef, and Purina ONE Chicken on hand. Try whichever he'll accept.
Moki is extremely picky
Moki will sometimes decide he's done with a food he's eaten happily for weeks and completely refuse it. We continuously rotate his wet food to stay ahead of this. Check what's in the pantry — there should be a few different options. If he's refusing one, try another brand or flavor before assuming something is wrong.

Feeding Schedule

Feed them around 8am and 8pm — same time as the dogs. The dry feeders handle themselves — you just need to put out wet food twice a day and give Moki his pill.

💊 Moki's Medication ▼

Moki takes Methimazole for his thyroid. This is important — don't skip doses.

Dosing

Medication Dose Frequency How to Give It
Methimazole 5mg (1 tablet) Twice daily (with AM + PM meals) Rub on the inner flap of his ear — alternate ears each dose. The pill bottle is from Costco Pharmacy. Note: the label says ½ tablet but the vet updated the dose to 1 full tablet.
About Methimazole
Methimazole controls hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid). Without it, Moki's heart rate goes up, he loses weight, and can get pretty miserable. It's a long-term medication, not something we're trying to wean him off of.
If he refuses to eat
Because Moki is so picky, there will be times he just won't eat a meal. The medication is a transdermal gel rubbed on his ear — you can apply it regardless of whether he eats. If he's skipping meals, try a different wet food flavor, or add a splash of hot water to his wet food and stir it in — the warm smell often gets him interested. If he goes more than 24 hours without eating, let us know.
🪣 Litter Box ▼

Both cats use a Litter Robot in the laundry area (same room where they're fed). It's a self-cleaning automatic litter box — it handles the scooping itself.

Your Only Job: Empty the Collection Tray

  • Pull out the waste drawer at the bottom and empty it into the trash every 2–4 days, depending on how full it looks.
  • If the blue light is flashing, the drawer needs to be emptied.
  • No scooping needed — the Litter Robot does that automatically after each use.

Litter Refill

There's a litter hopper on top that automatically keeps the bowl filled. Just make sure there's Fresh Step clumping litter in the hopper — if it's getting low, top it off. You shouldn't need to worry about this much during a normal stay.

Heads up — Moki's digestive issues
Moki has significant digestive sensitivities. His poops are often extremely stinky — more than you'd expect from a small cat. This is completely normal for him, not a sign something's wrong. The Litter Robot handles it, but just be aware when emptying the drawer.
🩺 Health Notes ▼

Maui 13 lbs

Maui was born in June 2009 — she's almost 17 and doing remarkably well for her age. A few things to know:

  • Allergies: She has environmental/food allergies — hence the Hill's Science Diet Sensitive Stomach & Skin food. Don't switch her dry food.
  • Overweight: She's on the chunky side. Don't give her extra food, treats, or let her eat Moki's food. We're trying to keep her weight stable, not actively diet her right now.
  • Early-stage kidney disease: Currently minor and being monitored. She's not on a prescription kidney diet yet, but avoid high-phosphorus treats (no deli meat, no fish-heavy treats). Keep fresh water available at all times — cats with kidney issues need to stay hydrated.

Moki 8 lbs

Moki was born around June 2011. He's a small, wiry cat and has had a few health things going on for years:

  • Hyperthyroidism: Managed with Methimazole (see the medication section). When well-controlled, he's fine. Signs it's not controlled: weight loss, increased appetite, hyperactivity, vomiting.
  • Early-stage kidney disease: Same as Maui — minor, being monitored. Fresh water is important.
  • Digestive sensitivities: His gut is just sensitive. Stinky poops are normal for him. Loose stools occasionally happen too — if it's one-off, not a concern. If it's persistent or he's clearly uncomfortable, let us know.
  • Extreme pickiness: He will sometimes completely stop eating a food he previously loved. This is not illness — it's just Moki being Moki. We keep several food options on hand. If he won't eat one thing, try another.
Water
Both cats have early-stage kidney disease, so keeping them hydrated matters. They share a pet fountain with the dogs in the kitchen area — just make sure it's running and topped off.
🏠 For House Sitters ▼

Hey! Thanks for taking care of the cats. Here's the short version of everything you need to know.

Daily Routine

  1. Feed both cats twice a day (morning and evening) — see amounts in the quick reference above.
  2. Give Moki his Methimazole with both meals — rub the gel on the inner flap of his ear (alternate ears). See the medication section for details.
  3. Empty the Litter Robot drawer every 2–4 days, or when the blue light flashes. No scooping needed — it cleans itself.
  4. Keep the kitchen fountain running — cats and dogs share it, and staying hydrated matters especially for the cats.

Key Things to Know

  • Keep their bowls separate. Maui will eat Moki's food if you let her, and Moki needs to eat his own food for the medication to work.
  • Moki may refuse a food. If he won't eat what's out, try a different flavor or brand from the options we left. You can also add a splash of hot water to his wet food and stir it in — the warm smell often gets him interested.
  • Maui is friendly and low-maintenance. She'll probably want lap time. She may beg for food — don't give her extra.
  • Moki is more skittish and may hide for a day or two while he adjusts to you being there. That's normal. He'll come out eventually.

What to Watch For

  • Moki refusing to eat for more than 24 hours
  • Moki vomiting repeatedly or seeming lethargic
  • Either cat avoiding the Litter Robot (may indicate a hardware issue)
  • Either cat clearly in distress
